The classic Pomodoro Technique tells you to work for 25 minutes, break for 5, and repeat. It's clean, simple, and it works — for a lot of people. But here's the thing: Francesco Cirillo invented that ratio as a university student in the late 1980s, timing himself with a [tomato-shaped kitchen timer](https://www.pomodorotechnique.com/). His context was studying. Yours might be writing code, designing interfaces, drafting a business plan, or plowing through emails.

The 25-minute pomodoro is a great starting point, not a universal law. The real power of the technique isn't the specific number — it's the principle of working in focused, timed intervals with intentional breaks. Once you understand the science behind it, you can adjust your pomodoro settings to match the kind of work you actually do.

## Why 25 Minutes Isn't Magic

Cirillo didn't arrive at 25 minutes through lab research. He started with just 10-minute focus sessions and gradually increased the duration as he improved his ability to concentrate. He eventually settled on 25 minutes as his sweet spot, partly influenced by [Tony Buzan's recommendation](https://pomodo.io/blog/who-is-francesco-cirillo/) of 20–40 minute study periods with breaks in between.

That range — 20 to 40 minutes — is significant. It roughly aligns with what attention researchers have found about sustained focus. A [study published in *Cognition*](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21211793/) by Ariga and Lleras (2011) demonstrated that prolonged attention to a single task leads to a "vigilance decrement" — your performance steadily drops. But brief mental switches (even momentary ones) were enough to reset focus and prevent that decline. The takeaway: it's not about powering through for as long as possible. It's about finding the right interval before your attention fades, then taking a genuine break.

So 25 minutes works for many people because it falls within that natural attention window. But depending on your task, your experience level, and your environment, a different interval might serve you better.

## The Science of Custom Pomodoro Intervals

Several lines of research point toward different optimal timer lengths, depending on the situation.

### The 90-Minute Cycle

Sleep researcher Nathaniel Kleitman proposed the [Basic Rest-Activity Cycle (BRAC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_rest%E2%80%93activity_cycle) — the idea that humans cycle through roughly 90 minutes of higher alertness followed by about 20 minutes of lower energy. This ultradian rhythm operates during the day, not just at night. Your heart rate, hormonal levels, and brain-wave activity all rise during the first part of the cycle and start declining after about an hour.

Tony Schwartz, author of *The Power of Full Engagement*, built his entire productivity philosophy around this rhythm. He wrote his two most recent books in [three 90-minute sprints per day](https://mariashriver.com/tony-schwartz-relax-be-more-productive/) with breaks in between — a total of about four and a half hours of focused work. Cal Newport makes a [similar recommendation](https://calnewport.com/on-deep-breaks/) for deep work: start with sessions under 90 minutes and gradually build up.

For complex, creative, or deeply analytical work, a 90-minute block might be the optimal timer length — if you can sustain focus that long.

### The 52/17 Rule

In 2014, time-tracking company DeskTime analyzed their user data and [found that their most productive users](https://desktime.com/blog/52-17-updated) worked in cycles of roughly 52 minutes on, 17 minutes off. These weren't people following any particular technique — the pattern emerged naturally from how top performers actually worked.

Interestingly, when DeskTime [repeated the study in 2021](https://desktime.com/blog/post-pandemic-productivity), the numbers had shifted: the most productive workers were now clocking 112-minute work sessions followed by 26-minute breaks. Work patterns had evolved, likely due to remote work and fewer office interruptions.

### The Short Sprint (15–20 Minutes)

On the other end, some people find that even 25 minutes is too long — especially for tasks that are boring, anxiety-inducing, or unfamiliar. If you're procrastinating on something, a 15-minute timer can lower the psychological barrier to starting. It's much easier to commit to "just 15 minutes" than to a full pomodoro.

This isn't a weakness. It's a legitimate strategy, and it's particularly effective for [beating procrastination through timeboxing](/blog/stop-procrastinating-timeboxing).

## How to Find Your Optimal Timer Length

There's no formula that spits out a perfect interval for everyone. But you can find yours through a structured experiment.

### Step 1: Categorize Your Work

Different tasks need different intervals. Start by sorting your work into buckets:

- **Deep creative/analytical work** — writing, coding, designing, problem-solving
- **Routine tasks** — email, admin, data entry, scheduling
- **Learning and studying** — reading, note-taking, practicing new skills
- **Communication-heavy work** — meetings, reviews, collaborative sessions

### Step 2: Start With a Baseline

For each category, pick a starting interval:

| Work Type | Starting Interval | Break |
|---|---|---|
| Deep work | 45–50 min | 10–15 min |
| Routine tasks | 25 min (classic) | 5 min |
| Learning | 25–30 min | 5–10 min |
| Communication | 30–40 min | 10 min |

These aren't rules — they're starting points based on the research above. The classic 25/5 works well for routine tasks because the time pressure keeps you moving. Deep work benefits from longer sessions because it takes 10–15 minutes just to get into a [flow state](/blog/science-of-flow-state).

### Step 3: Run a One-Week Experiment

For one week, stick to your chosen intervals and track two things:

1. **How focused were you** during the session? (1–5 scale)
2. **How did you feel** when the timer went off? Were you still in the zone, or were you already fading?

If you consistently feel like you're hitting your stride right as the timer rings, your interval is too short. If you're checking the clock at the 15-minute mark, it's too long.

### Step 4: Adjust by 5–10 Minutes

Make small adjustments. If 25 minutes felt too short for your coding sessions, try 35. If 50 minutes left you drained, drop to 40. Small increments help you zero in on the right balance without overshooting.

## Practical Pomodoro Settings for Common Scenarios

Here are interval configurations that many people find effective, based on the research and common feedback from productivity communities.

### For Software Developers

- **Work interval**: 45–50 minutes
- **Short break**: 10 minutes
- **Long break**: 20–30 minutes after 2–3 sessions

Coding often requires holding complex mental models in your head. A 25-minute pomodoro can feel interruptive when you're debugging or architecting a solution. Longer sessions give you time to load context, do the work, and reach a natural stopping point. Check out our [guide to the Pomodoro Technique for developers](/blog/pomodoro-technique-for-developers) for more specific strategies.

### For Writers and Content Creators

- **Work interval**: 30–45 minutes
- **Short break**: 10 minutes
- **Long break**: 20 minutes after 3 sessions

Writing is cognitively intense but often benefits from a rhythm of sprint and pause. If you do creative work like writing, design, or music, our guide on [using the Pomodoro Technique for creative work](/blog/pomodoro-for-creative-work) goes deeper into interval strategies tailored to the creative process. The break gives your subconscious time to process ideas — many writers find that solutions to tricky passages come during breaks, not during the session itself.

### For Students

- **Work interval**: 25 minutes (classic)
- **Short break**: 5 minutes
- **Long break**: 15–20 minutes after 4 sessions

The original Pomodoro intervals were designed for studying, and they still work well. The shorter duration keeps material fresh and prevents that glazed-over feeling when you've been staring at notes too long. If you're doing practice problems or active recall, 25 minutes is usually enough for a productive set.

### For Administrative and Email Work

- **Work interval**: 20–25 minutes
- **Short break**: 5 minutes

Admin tasks benefit from short, high-urgency sprints. The timer creates artificial scarcity — you'll be surprised how much email you can clear when you know the clock is ticking.

### For Creative Brainstorming

- **Work interval**: 15–20 minutes
- **Short break**: 5–10 minutes

Shorter bursts work well for brainstorming because creative ideation thrives on energy and spontaneity. Longer sessions tend to produce diminishing returns as you start filtering ideas instead of generating them.

## The Break Matters as Much as the Work

One mistake people make when customizing their pomodoro intervals: they extend the work session but keep the break the same. If you're working for 50 minutes instead of 25, a 5-minute break isn't enough.

A rough guideline: your break should be about 15–20% of your work session. So:

- 25-minute work → 5-minute break (20%)
- 45-minute work → 8–10-minute break (~20%)
- 90-minute work → 15–20-minute break (~20%)

And a real break means stepping away from the screen. The DeskTime research specifically noted that the most productive workers weren't checking email or scrolling social media during their breaks — they were [physically stepping away from the computer](https://desktime.com/blog/52-17-updated).

## When to Adjust (and When Not To)

**Do adjust your intervals when:**

- You consistently lose focus before the timer ends
- You're always in a flow state when it rings
- Your work type changes (e.g., switching from coding to meetings)
- You're returning from a break or vacation and need to rebuild focus stamina

**Don't adjust your intervals when:**

- You've had one bad session — that happens to everyone
- You're trying to avoid the discomfort of focus itself (that's resistance, not a settings problem)
- You haven't given a configuration at least a full week of testing

The goal of custom pomodoro intervals isn't to find a way to avoid hard focus. It's to find the cadence that lets you sustain it. The right interval should feel slightly challenging but not exhausting — like a workout at the right intensity.

## The Bottom Line

The Pomodoro Technique is a framework, not a prescription. The 25-minute interval is where you start, not where you have to stay. The research is clear: different work demands different rhythms. A [90-minute deep work session](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_rest%E2%80%93activity_cycle) and a 15-minute email sprint are both valid uses of timed focus — as long as you're intentional about why you chose that length.

Experiment with your [Pomodorian settings](https://pomodorian.app), track what works, and iterate. The best pomodoro interval is the one that keeps you coming back for the next session.
